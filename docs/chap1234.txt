BACKGROUND AND EXISTING WORK 
Traditionally, therapists helped people with speech difficulties using hands-on techniques, focusing on exercises to control speech muscles. These were like special workouts to make talking easier. With technology getting smarter, there are new gadgets using learning tricks to help, but they struggle to sound natural and handle all speech challenges in real-time. 
Recent tech advances brought in devices using machine learning to assist with speech issues. However, they face challenges in providing immediate and natural-sounding support for different ways people speak. Our project aims to bridge this gap by combining the wisdom of traditional speech therapy with the latest machine learning tricks. It's like mixing the best from the past with the coolest tech now. 
Building on what others have done, our project sets out to create a system that not only understands and adjusts to challenging speech but also makes talking feel more natural and special for each person. Our goal is to address the changing needs in helping people with speech difficulties, making communication a smoother and more personalized experience for those with dysarthria. 
2.1. Literature Review 
The study in [1] by Goudar, Swain, and Naik presents a novel dysarthria recognition and speech reconstruction system, enhancing communication for individuals with dysarthria through advanced speech recognition and reconstruction technologies. 
In [2], Dinh, Nguyen, and Park introduce a deep learning-based dysarthria voice reconstruction system using transfer learning. This system improves speech intelligibility for dysarthric patients by fine-tuning pre-trained models, making it effective even with limited dysarthric data. 
The work in [3] by Aihara, Takiguchi, and Ariki focuses on phoneme-discriminative features for dysarthric speech conversion. Their research enhances speech recognition by developing features tailored to the phonetic characteristics of dysarthric speech, improving conversion 
accuracy. 
Chen et al. in [4] develop a joint-feature learning-based voice conversion system for dysarthric users. Utilizing deep learning, this system adapts to the vocal characteristics of dysarthric speech, resulting in clearer communication. 
8 
Yang and Chung in [5] use cycle-consistent adversarial training to improve the intelligibility of dysarthric speech. Their approach enhances clarity while maintaining naturalness. 
VocaliD, as discussed in [6], creates personalized voices for individuals with speech impairments, allowing users to generate voices that reflect their identity. This innovation empowers users to communicate more naturally. 
In [7]. Acapela Group's "My-own-voice" service provides personalized synthetic voices for individuals with speech challenges. This service enables users to create voices that resemble their own, enhancing communication and identity retention. 
Shor et al. in [8] focus on personalizing automatic speech recognition (ASR) for dysarthric and accented speech with limited data. Their machine learning techniques improve ASR performance for users with speech impairments, making voice technology more accessible. 
Enderby's review in [9] explores dysarthria and its communication challenges, offering insights into the complexities of the disorder and emphasizing the need for targeted interventions to improve communication. 
Finally, Fu et al. in [10] apply joint dictionary learning-based non-negative matrix factorization for voice conversion, improving speech intelligibility after oral surgery. Their work demonstrates the potential of signal processing in speech rehabilitation.

2.4. Comparison of Existing Systems 
Our project, which converts dysarthric speech to text and generates machine voices, 
has broader goals than the adversarial speaker adaptation (ASA) approach. ASA primarily 
focuses on preserving speaker identity in dysarthric speech reconstruction, while our project 
addresses both speech-to-text conversion and voice generation. VocaliD and My-Own-Voice 
focus on personalized synthetic voices and voice cloning, with less emphasis on the specifics of dysarthric speech. Our project covers a wider scope, tackling both text conversion and voice generation, while ASA centers on identity preservation during speech reconstruction.

In this phase, the VGGish component captures audio features from the provided dysarthric speech samples. 
• The extracted features are then forwarded to the Dysarthria Detection 
Algorithm for dysarthria assessment.

2. Dysarthria Detection Stage 
•

Based on the analysis, it generates dysarthria status or classification, indicating whether the speech is dysarthric or not. 
3. Text Conversion Stage 
• Upon identifying dysarthric speech, the system passes the audio to the Speech-to-Text component. 
• The Speech-to-Text System translates the dysarthric speech into textual representation for further processing. 
4. Machine Voice Synthesis Stage 
The generated text undergoes processing in the Text-to-Speech System for converting it into a machine-generated voice. 
• The resulting machine voice output is delivered back to the user or as the final output of the system.
